📘 Linear Regression Concept

What is Linear Regression?

Linear regression is a mathematical and statistical model that aims to explain the relationship between a dependent variable (Y) and one or more independent variables (X), fitting a straight line that best represents that relationship.

Its basic equation is:

Y = β₀ + β₁X + ε

Where:

  • Y = the variable you want to predict
  • X = the variable that influences Y
  • β₀ = intercept (the value of Y when X = 0)
  • β₁ = slope (how much Y changes when X increases by 1 unit)
  • ε = error or noise

What is it used for?

Linear regression is used to:

  1. Predict future values: Estimate sales based on advertising, income based on years of study, or the price of a house based on its size.
  2. Measure the impact of one variable on another: How much a product’s value increases when more money is invested in marketing.
  3. Identify patterns or trends: Growth trends in energy consumption.
  4. Evaluate causal relationships or correlations: Does temperature affect ice cream demand?

Linear regression is one of the most widely used techniques in Machine Learning because of its simplicity, interpretability, and performance.

Real Case Study (Well Explained)

Use Case: Predicting future inventory of products

Very useful for your inventory management project.

Problem: A store wants to anticipate how many units of a product it will need next week.

Available data:

  • Sales from recent weeks
  • Day of the month
  • Month of the year
  • Season (holidays, promotions)

Assume we use simple linear regression with:

  • X = sales from last week
  • Y = sales for next week

The model learns a relationship such as:

Sales_next_week = 5 + 0.8 × Sales_last_week

Interpretation:

If last week the store sold 100 units, the model predicts:

5 + 0.8 × 100 = 85 units

This helps plan purchases, avoid stockouts, and prevent overstock.

Classic Case Study (Very Clear)

Predicting the price of a house

Variables:

  • X: square meters
  • Y: house price

If the model learns:

Price = 20,000 + 1,000 × SquareMeters

Interpretation:

Each additional square meter adds $1,000 to the price.

A house with 80 m² would cost:

20,000 + 1,000 × 80 = 100,000

Simple, interpretable, and useful.
